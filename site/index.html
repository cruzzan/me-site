About me

Growing up in the Swedish countryside not too far from Gothenburg, I developed an instinct for tinkering and problem-solving early on. Building tree houses and helping my parents with farmwork and upkeep made it central to everyday life. A three-year stint in Melbourne during my teens shaped me in ways that still show up at work: an ease with new people, new contexts, and an English that has never felt like a second language.

My start

I was building things well before I knew what I was doing. When my cousin brought me in to work with him in 2011, I jumped at the chance. Not because I had a plan, but because I wanted to be in the thick of it. Getting things working. Learning from what broke.

That period taught me something that has stayed with me ever since: the difference between hacking something together and actually understanding what you've built. I could make things work, but I struggled to reason about why. And that gap bothered me enough to go back to school for it.

It's a pattern I keep returning to. Not just solving the problem in front of me, but understanding the system well enough to see what the next one will be.

What I value

The work I find most satisfying is when I can trace a problem from the customer's pain all the way through to the code that solves it. Not just implementing a spec, but understanding why it matters and what a good outcome actually looks like.

I have seen what happens when engineers are kept at arm's length from the product context. The solutions tend to be technically correct and practically wrong. The engineers who make the biggest impact are the ones who are curious about the whole system, including the parts they are not directly responsible for. That curiosity is what lets you catch problems early and make decisions that age well.

I do my best work in smaller teams where that kind of ownership is possible: building something, watching it land with real users, and knowing whether it actually worked.

Outside of work

Away from the keyboard, I run, cook, play golf, and ski. The thing I spend the most time on, though, is renovations and hands-on building. In my own home and for friends and family, usually more ambitious than it should be. But it scratches the same itch as programming: scoping out a problem, working through the ones you did not anticipate, and ending up with something tangible.

  • 2023.12 - Sendify - Senior software engineer

    Senior software engineer2023 - Now

    Sendify AB

    When I joined, Sendify was at an inflection point. The platform had been built quickly, and the tech had kept pace with that, fast and pragmatic. A pair of major rebuilds were being finished as I arrived: a full replacement of the invoicing system, and bringing the pricing and product compatibility logic in-house, after years of outsourcing it. Our efforts were focused on getting those initiatives across the line while managing any other issues from earlier technical choices that the business had simply outgrown. All of this while growth and new development had slowed below where we wanted it.

    My early work involved assisting the leading engineers on the project of in-housing the pricing engine. This mainly meant making changes and improvements to carrier integrations so that our existing catalog of carriers became compatible, one by one. This helped me gain a deep understanding of how the system worked, and how complicated our role in the supply chain is. And how hard it is to get a product set up with high accuracy. Not to mention keeping it accurate with continuous pricing and policy changes from the carriers.

    These factors of uncertainty made it difficult for us to balance our own exposure to risk with our reliability towards our customers. Which I found to be a very interesting area of improvement, so I led the subteam focusing on this area. We built tooling to preview any updates to orders as they were being prepared to be invoiced, allowing us to catch problems early. Then we added custom-rules based analysis to help figure out what was causing the updates. And eventually automating corrections to address any known issues without intervention. These efforts cut invoicing tickets in half, and reduced the rate of customer churn attributed to invoicing issues.

    I also led a project to open up a segment of customers we had previously been unable to serve due to high credit risk. The solution was a direct payment flow at the point of booking, which was new territory for us, since the platform had always been invoice-only. The driver was clear: we were losing roughly 20% of new organic growth in the German market to low or incomplete credit scores. Initial live testing showed that we could convert roughly half of those previously blocked customers into active weekly shippers.
